## How It Works

The solution is derived from the geometric properties of a cone and the definition of Steiner's generation of a conic section.

### 1\. Geometric Setup 📐

We place the right-circular cone in a 3D coordinate system with its **apex at the origin (0, 0, 0)** and its central axis along the Z-axis.

  * The equation of this cone is $x^2 + y^2 = k^2 z^2$, where $k$ is the constant slope of the cone's surface, defined as $k = \\frac{\\text{baseRadius}}{\\text{height}} = \\frac{r}{h}$.
  * The **vertical cutting plane** is parallel to the Z-axis and is given by the equation $x = d$, where $d$ is the `offset`.
  * The **base of the cone** lies on the horizontal plane defined by $z = h$.

### 2\. Finding the Asymptotes

The Steiner points for this construction are the points where the **asymptotes of the hyperbola intersect the base of the cone**.

To find the asymptotes, we first find the equation of the hyperbola by substituting the plane's equation ($x=d$) into the cone's equation:

$$d^2 + y^2 = \left(\frac{r}{h}\right)^2 z^2$$

Rearranging this into the standard form for a hyperbola gives:

$$\left(\frac{r}{h}\right)^2 z^2 - y^2 = d^2$$

The asymptotes of this hyperbola are found by setting the constant term to zero:

$$\left(\frac{r}{h}\right)^2 z^2 - y^2 = 0 \implies y^2 = \left(\frac{r}{h}\right)^2 z^2 \implies y = \pm \frac{r}{h} z$$

These two lines ($y = \\frac{r}{h}z$ and $y = -\\frac{r}{h}z$) represent the asymptotes on the plane $x=d$.

### 3\. Intersection with the Cone's Base

Finally, we find where these asymptote lines intersect the cone's base plane, $z=h$. We simply substitute $z=h$ into the asymptote equations:

$$y = \pm \frac{r}{h} (h) \implies y = \pm r$$

This gives us the y-coordinates of the Steiner points. We already know their x and z coordinates from the definitions of the cutting plane and the base plane.

Therefore, the coordinates of the two Steiner points are:

  * **Point 1:** $(d, r, h)$ which corresponds to `(offset, baseRadius, height)`
  * **Point 2:** $(d, -r, h)$ which corresponds to `(offset, -baseRadius, height)`

This elegant result shows that the calculation is surprisingly simple, as reflected in the JavaScript function.

## How the Point Generation Works

The Steiner generation of a conic can be seen as finding the intersection of corresponding lines from two projective pencils. In our 3D case, a practical way to apply this principle is to find the intersection of the cone's generator lines with the cutting plane. For robust and clean computation, we first derive the hyperbola's equation and then use a parametric formula to trace its path.

### 1\. The Hyperbola's Equation

As shown previously, the intersection of the cone $x^2 + y^2 = (r/h)^2 z^2$ and the plane $x=d$ results in the hyperbola defined by:

$$\left(\frac{r}{h}\right)^2 z^2 - y^2 = d^2$$

This equation describes the shape of the hyperbola on the 2D plane where $x$ is fixed at $d$.

### 2\. Parametric Formula using Hyperbolic Functions 📈

To generate points along this curve in a controlled way, we use a parametrization based on hyperbolic trigonometric functions, which are the natural way to describe a hyperbola, much like `sin` and `cos` describe a circle.

We can satisfy the equation above by setting:

  * $y = d \\sinh(u)$
  * $\\frac{r}{h} z = d \\cosh(u)$

Here, $u$ is a parameter that we can vary. Let's check if this works by substituting it back into the equation:

$$\left(d \cosh(u)\right)^2 - \left(d \sinh(u)\right)^2 = d^2$$
$$d^2 (\cosh^2(u) - \sinh^2(u)) = d^2$$

Because of the fundamental identity $\\cosh^2(u) - \\sinh^2(u) = 1$, this simplifies to $d^2 = d^2$. The identity holds, confirming our parametrization is correct.

### 3\. Final Point Coordinates

By rearranging the z-component and including our fixed x-coordinate, we get the 3D coordinates for any point on the hyperbola based on the parameter $u$:

  * $x = d$
  * $y = d \\sinh(u)$
  * $z = \\frac{d \\cdot h}{r} \\cosh(u)$

The function `generateHyperbolaPoints` simply loops from a negative to a positive value of `u` (`-parameterRange` to `+parameterRange`) to calculate the $(x, y, z)$ coordinates for `numPoints` points along the curve. The vertex of the hyperbola occurs at $u=0$.
